Planning a Grounded Theory Study

Mian Hu

2021-10-05 14:15:13

There are many qualitative studies seek to descibe and explore phenomena. Grounded theory approach differens from other approaches in that it serves to explain phenomena also. However, it does not mean that all grounded theory studies actually result in a theory that demonstrates explanatory power.

When is grounded theory appopriate?

  • Little is known about the area of study
  • The generation of theory with explanatory power is a desired outcome
  • An inherent process is imbedded in the research situation that is likely to be explicated by grounded theory methods

Grounded theory research can result in the generation of knowledge, so the research areas should not be extensively explored. It should also be noted that research designs with an interpretive component are not intended for generalisation. The research questions are in unique situations that cannot be addressed in other contexts.

When using grounded theory, you should also move beyond simple description of phenomena through exploration, as the aim of using grounded theory is to generate theories that explain phenomena. A question would be if the researcher should have the intention to generate a theory before conducting research. Even so, he or she can have some sort theoretical understanding, or may fail to do so. What kind of factors could influence the result? Or the quality of the grounded theory studies?

The third condition specifically mention process as an important characteristic feature of grounded theory. What is process in grounded theory? Corbin and Strauss (2008) define it as an ‘ ongoing action/emotion/interaction taken in response to situations, or problems.’ In this sense, one actually can also find ‘process’ in basic qualitative research.

Planning a grounded theory study

  1. Acknowledge assumptions

    Researchers often choose an area to study because of their passion for a topic or a personal interest stemming from experience. To generate theory, one has to have a balance between maintaining an open mind and also being able to identify concepts of theoretic imporatance during data collection and analysis. As Sturbing (2007) advises, this requires avoiding imposing personal preconceptions on the developing theory, also ensuring the knowledge and experience possessed is used effectively. This sounds quite difficult. Isolating my preconceptions from knowledge and experience. Aren’t my preconceptions also part of my experience and knowledge? The chapter also lists the ‘assumptions’ about your research need to identified.

    • Your philosophical position (how you see the world) and how it relates to both the topic area of your study and your application of grounded theory methods and principles (as you will have done through completing the activity in the previous chapter).
    • What you already know about the topic of your research, from both formal study and per- sonal/professional experience.
    • What you expect you will find from your research; while it is important not to influence the outcome, often it is only through acknowledging your predictions that such preconception can be avoided.
    • Any apprehension, concerns or fears you have in relation to your study and how your strengths and limitations may impact on the process.

  2. Clarify the research questions and aims

    Most research designs are directed by research questions. However, it is the research process that generates the question in grounded theory study. Glaser (1978) has consistantly referred to the concept of ‘emergence’ in relation to grounded theory outcomes. This seems to be contradictory to that the grounded theory researchers come with an desired outcome to generate theory. It is also suggested that one can have quite broad research questions and refine them early in the research process.

  3. Review the literature

    Literature can be used to enhance theoretical sensitivity; as data during analysis; and as source of theoretical codes.

  4. Develop a research design

  5. When eigning grounded theory research, it is possible to be frustrated by the evoling nature of this approach. Theoretical sampling will determine how data will be generated, from what sources and in what locations, as the study progresses. Taking my research as an example, I originaly planned to study novice teachers’ professional development. After collecting data from a questionnaire and face -to-face interview, my supervisor and I found that most teachers in this context are mainly novice Chinese female teachers, novice native male teachers, or experienced native teachers. We decided to focus on these groups rather than on novice teachers only.

  6. Identify ethical and legal issues

    Also consider:

    What privacy legislation may impact on your intention to access certain documents?

    What measures will you put in place to prevent, minimize or respond to distress in an interview situation when you do not know where the questions will lead?

  7. Determine required resources

    Consider potential sources of funding and assistance.

  8. Develop a timeline

    The most important is to build flexibility and contingency. There will be a lot of unexpected factors that could delay the process, i.e. IRBs require adittional information; gatekeepers may delay the access to data sources; participants may not be available, etc.

  9. Writing a formal proposal

When writig a formal proposal, it is important to demonstrate the relevance and significance of the proposed study, also the ability to conduct the study.

References:

Corbin, J., & Strauss, A. (2008). Strategies for qualitative data analysis. Basics of Qualitative Research. Techniques and procedures for developing grounded theory, 3.

Glaser, B. G. (1978). Theoretical sensitivity. Sociology Press, Mill Valley, CA.

Strübing, J. (2007). Research as pragmatic problem-solving: The pragmatist roots of empirically-grounded theorizing. The Sage handbook of grounded theory, 580-602.

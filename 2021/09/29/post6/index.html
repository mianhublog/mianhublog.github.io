The grounded theory generations

Barney Glaser and Aneslm Strauss worked on a study to examine the experience of dying. After the completion, they published The discovery of grounded theory, which marks the first generation of grounded theory. Theorists after them, the second generation, have worked towards interpreting Glaser and Strauss’s grounded theory. Table 1.1 lists the evolving timeline of grounded theory.

Philosophy, methodology and methods

What is the difference between a methodology and methods? Stemming from a congruent philosophy, a methodology is a set of principles and ideas that inform the design of a research study. Methods, on the other hand, are practical procedures or techniques used to generate and analyse data. The methodological framework with its underpinning philosophy influences how the researcher works with the participants,, in other words, how they position themselevs in the study, i.e., a position of distance or acknowledged inclusion in the study. In terms of the first generation grounded theory, it was criticised that they did not elaborate the theory at methodological leverl, but focused on the techniques used for conduct grounded theory study.

Methodological influences on grounded theory

  1. Qualitative research studies originate from early world explorers who document their experiences of encountering the tribes of foreign lands while collecting cultural artefacts, all in the name of colonization.
  2. The second moment (from the end of the Second World War to 1970) is known as the ‘golden age of rigorous qualitative analysis’ (Denzin & Lincoln, 2005: 16) during which time Glaser and Strauss developed grounded theory methods.
  3. The third moment of qualitative research dawned soon after the publication of Discovery of grounded theory, as a response to cultural ruptures in American society (2005). This phase is called blurred genres (1970–86) and is characterized by qualitative researchers questioning their place in research texts. Constructivist thinking became very influential in this moment.
  4. It was not until the fourth moment of qualitative research (2005), dubbed the crisis of representation (1986–95), that Charmaz began to publish about constructivist grounded theory (Charmaz, 1995).
  5. The fifth moment of qualitative research overlaps and extends the fourth and is termed the triple crisis as it adds legitimation and praxis to representation. Legitimation questioned measures for deciding on the merit of qualitative research outcomes, while the crisis of praxis provoked questions about the ability of textual analyses of society to effect change (Denzin & Lincoln, 2005).

Discerning a personal philosophical position

All research is interpretive; it is guided by the researcher’s set of beliefs and feelings about the world and how it should be understood and studied. Some beliefs may be taken for granted, invisible, only assumed, whereas others are highly problematic and controversial. (Denzin & Lincoln, 2005: 22).

  1. How do we define our self? Self is dynamic, social-constructed, accumulation of experiences.
  2. What is the nature of reality? There is not only one reality. There are realities defined by different people.
  3. What can be the relationship between researcher and participant? A researcher can distance himself or herself from participants as a total observer, or another participant in the study. From my perspective, a researcher can be absolutely distant from the study as qualitative research is researchers’ interpretations. Whether to distant themselves or be inclusive in the study depends on the purpose of the study.
  4. How do we know the world, or gain knowledge of it? We gain knowledge by doing, and reflecting on the experience. There is also non-direct knowledge we learn from books or previous generations.

Essential grounded theory methods

  1. Initial coding and categorization of data
  2. Construct data generation or collection and analysis
  3. Writing memos
  4. Theoretical sampling
  5. Constant comparative analysis
  6. Theoretical sensitivity
  7. Intermediate coding
  8. Identifying a core category
  9. Advanced coding and theoretical integration
  10. Generating theory

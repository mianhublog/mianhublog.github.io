Positivist Research, Death of the Self

Mian Hu

2021-09-21 15:25:29

Kress (2011) begins this chapter with her doctoral study experience when the professor sounded like, “when you do this kind of work, you believe these numbers mean something”. However, these words made her doubt the neutral role of researchers in positivistic research. It made a sense that positivism is actually a belief system. It is just one intervention designed by humans as a way of making sense of the world.

What Is Positivism?

The term was first coined by French philosopher Auguste Comte in the early nineteenth century. Comte believed that only scientific findings could be deemed certain, even social behaviour could be regarded as mechanistic and governed by underlying natural laws that were quantifiable and could be predicteed and manipulated. The tenets of positivism can be dated back to the time of Plato and Socrates. Age of Reason and Enlightenment thinkers of the 17th and 18th centuries furthered these ideas. Enlightment thinking can be summarized as follows:

  • There is a singular, stable, “true” reality external to man that can be perceived by one’s senses (realism).
  • Complex phenomena occurring in this reality can best be explained and appre- ciated by reducing them to their constituent parts and then piecing them back together based on causal laws (reductionism).
  • Thereisaninternalsubjectiveworldofsensationandanexternalobjectiveworld of phenomena and these two things are separate (Cartesian dualism).

Scientism and the Myth of the Scientific Method

Cartesian dualism (or the separation of the knower from the known) and the use of mathematics and reductionism to discover universal truths have been very influential in Western philosophy and modern science. The popular conception of the scientific method involves the following

  1. observing a phenomenon in the natural world
  2. formulating a hypothesis about the nature of that phenomenon
  3. using this hypothesis to theorize about or predict the outcome of the occurrence of the phenomenon
  4. performing an experiment to test the accuracy of this hypothesis
  5. making conclusions based on observation of the results of the experiment

Operationally, the scientific method can be attibuted to Francis Bacon who made explicit advocates for the use in observing natural phenomena. Galileo and Newton epitomized the latter. At the grand age of science, the scientific method seemed to gain popularity in the humanity fields. In applying the scientific method, social life, just as the natural world,can be understood mathematically through causal, invariable laws, and their interrelations if one would simply use the proper method of observing, experimenting, and predicting. In conducting empirical research about people, one must

  • pose salient research questions,
  • identify important variables,
  • obtain measures for all participants on allv ariables,and
  • analyze data to show causal relationships between variables.

However, this scientific method is a very unnatural way whe making sense of people, since people and their social contexts are complex and in flux. It is impossible to identify the variables to unpack a phenomenon. Besides, is the scientific method constantly reliable or unbaised? The reliability of textbook science is not attributable to the scientific method itself, but rather to the long-standing consensus among scientists. However, because mistakes do not make it into the science textbooks, the scientific method often seems infallible (Bauer, 1992). Also, scientists cannot be disinterested observers as many of them receive funding from public or private organisations.

The Rise of Positivism in Educational Research and Reform

In the field of education in the United States, the rise of positivism has been a non-linear process.

Time The Rise of Positivism
19th century Post-Darwinian ideas, science led to innovation and progress; “laboratory” style research; the application of psychology to education
20th century more practical applications of psychology to education; schools were seen as places to “fix” people and in turn “fix” society; surveys became a means for policy elites
1920s “increasingly quantitative”; the Testing Movement fueled by the development of IQ tests
1950s-1960s increase funding for education, particularly toward strengthening mathematics and science programs
1970s-1990s cognitive science starts to loosen the behaviorist stranglehold; more attention to the social contexts in which children were learning; rise of qualitative, ethnographic, and practice-based teacher research
21st century positivism and scientism have become further entrenched; educational research and reform often with references to empir- ical evidence, data-driven assessments, standards, and accountability

Exposing the Ghosts of Positivism

Measuring of inputs and outputs via standard testing is one of the most obvious positivistic ghosts, which has been popular for determing program reform, resource allocation, adoption of curricula, teacher hiring and promotion, student tracking, etc. On the surface, this type of thinking, measuring the effectiveness by measuring the level of achievement gains of students, seems logical; however, drawing simplistic cause and effect relationships such as this can have disastrous consequences. Quantitative data can mean many things to different people based upon their own lived experiences, values, and political aims. Thus, the very gathering of this kind of decontextualized data could have disastrous consequences for marginalized groups because of the ways in which data can be used for domination, i.e., the Coleman Repor as evidence that African-American children were inferior to their white peers; claimes made that the report showed equal opportunitiy is not effective for African-American school children. Meanwhile, feminist scholar Harding (1998) argues that positivism has had dehumanizing consequences for women because of its Eurocentric and therefore androcentric focus.

Although positivism is supposedly “dead”, it still influences the decisions we make about education and research, often in dangerous, unjust ways.

Preventing “Intellectual Suicide” with CPR

Kress (2011) says removing ourselves from our work is asking us to commit “intellectual suicide.” By pretending to be “objective”—we “kill off” all those things that make each of our lives uniquely and wonderfully human.

Critical Praxis Researchers are those who seek to conduct research that is emancipatory and humanizing; therefore, we accept that we cannot hide from ourselves because we cannot hide from life, nor do we want to.
